# Viral Hooks Skill

## 1. Overview

This skill governs the creation of scroll-stopping opening lines for social
media posts and threads. The hook is the single most important line in any
post — it determines whether someone reads the rest or keeps scrolling.
This skill provides the strategist and writer agents with 12 proven hook
frameworks, platform-specific adaptation rules, and a systematic process
for drafting, testing, and selecting hooks.

Activate this skill whenever creating opening lines for any social media
content across LinkedIn, X, TikTok, or Facebook.

## 2. Core Capabilities

- 12 hook frameworks with psychological triggers and templates
- Platform-specific "above the fold" character budgets
- Hook intensity calibration matched to content weight
- Hook-to-content alignment validation
- A/B variant generation (minimum 3 per post)
- Anti-clickbait enforcement (promise must match delivery)
- Emotional layering techniques for maximum stopping power
- Read-aloud validation for naturalness

## 3. When to Use This Skill

| Trigger | Action |
|---------|--------|
| Strategist generates hook ideas for calendar | Apply framework selection |
| Writer drafts post opening lines | Draft 3+ variants using different frameworks |
| Optimizer reviews a post's hook | Validate intensity, alignment, and platform fit |
| Post underperforms on engagement | Analyze hook and suggest rewrite with different framework |
| Thread is being created | Apply thread-specific hook + mini-hook structure |

## 4. Detailed Workflow

### Step 1: Analyze the Content

Before writing a hook, understand what the post delivers:
- **Core message:** What is the single takeaway?
- **Proof quality:** What evidence supports the claim? (numbers, stories, examples)
- **Audience:** Who needs to stop scrolling for this?
- **Objective:** Awareness, engagement, traffic, or conversion?
- **Content weight:** Major announcement, educational tip, community discussion, or personal reflection?

### Step 2: Select 3 Hook Frameworks

Choose 3 different frameworks from the 12 below. Draft one variant per
framework. Never submit a single hook — always provide options.

**Framework 1: The Contrarian**
Challenge conventional wisdom. Force the reader to reconsider an assumption.

Template: `Everyone says [X]. They're wrong. Here's why.`

Psychology: Cognitive dissonance — when beliefs are challenged, people engage
to defend or update their worldview.

Examples:
- "Stop following best practices. They're killing your results."
- "Everyone says you need a co-founder. I disagree."
- "The worst advice in agritech: 'just scale up.'"

Best for: LinkedIn thought leadership, X hot takes.
Risk: High — must have strong reasoning in the body to back the claim.

**Framework 2: The Proof**
Lead with a specific, verifiable result or number.

Template: `We went from [before] to [after] in [timeframe]. Here's how.`

Psychology: Specificity equals credibility. Vague claims are ignored;
precise numbers demand attention.

Examples:
- "We went from 0 to 10K users in 30 days. No ads."
- "This one change cut our post-harvest loss from 30% to 6%."
- "340 farms. 14 months. 34% average revenue increase."

Best for: All platforms. Universally high-performing framework.
Risk: Low — as long as the numbers are real and defensible.

**Framework 3: The Question**
Trigger curiosity with an open question the reader can't ignore.

Template: `What if everything you know about [X] is wrong?`

Psychology: Open loops — the brain wants closure. An unanswered question
creates tension that can only be resolved by reading the post.

Examples:
- "Why do 90% of farm-to-market startups fail in year two?"
- "What's the one thing successful farmers never talk about?"
- "How much produce did you lose last season? Be honest."

Best for: Facebook (questions drive highest engagement), LinkedIn.
Risk: Low — but avoid obviously rhetorical questions that feel hollow.

**Framework 4: The Story**
Start in the middle of the action. Drop the reader into a scene.

Template: `At [time/place], [vivid situation]. What happened next changed everything.`

Psychology: Narrative transportation — the reader is pulled into the story
and must keep reading to find out what happens.

Examples:
- "4 AM. Rain pouring. 200kg of tomatoes that had to move today."
- "Last Tuesday, our delivery truck broke down 40km from the market."
- "I sat across from a buyer who told me my produce wasn't worth the drive."

Best for: LinkedIn, Facebook (story-friendly platforms).
Risk: Low — stories are inherently engaging if they're real.

**Framework 5: The List Promise**
Promise a specific number of actionable items.

Template: `[Number] [things] that [outcome]. Number [X] is [teaser].`

Psychology: Lists reduce cognitive load and set clear expectations.
The teaser for one item creates a targeted curiosity gap.

Examples:
- "7 tools every AI engineer needs in 2026 (most are free)."
- "3 mistakes I see every startup make with LLMs."
- "5 features we built because farmers asked. Here's the unexpected one."

Best for: LinkedIn, X threads. Universal performer.
Risk: Low — list posts are safe and consistent. Risk is overuse.

**Framework 6: The Hot Take**
Express a strong, defensible opinion that will polarize.

Template: `[Strong opinion]. And I'll defend it.`

Psychology: Polarization drives engagement on both sides. Agreement
generates shares; disagreement generates comments. Both feed algorithms.

Examples:
- "RAG is dead. Here's what replaces it."
- "Farmers markets are overrated. Here's what actually works."
- "You don't need a framework. You need 50 lines of Python."

Best for: X (rewards opinion), LinkedIn (thought leadership).
Risk: High — hot takes must be genuinely held beliefs with reasoning.

**Framework 7: The Curiosity Gap**
Reveal part of an insight but withhold the key detail.

Template: `I tried [action] for [timeframe]. The result was [intriguing but vague].`

Psychology: The Zeigarnik effect — incomplete information creates tension
that can only be resolved by continuing to read.

Examples:
- "We changed one thing about how we package produce. Revenue doubled."
- "I asked 50 farmers the same question. Their answers surprised me."
- "There's a reason your produce doesn't sell at market. It's not quality."

Best for: All platforms. Strong on TikTok (drives watch-through).
Risk: Medium — the payoff must match the setup. Over-hyped gaps are clickbait.

**Framework 8: The Before/After**
Present a dramatic transformation with specific metrics.

Template: `[Before state] → [After state]. Here's what changed.`

Psychology: Transformation stories trigger aspiration. The reader sees
themselves in the "before" and wants to reach the "after."

Examples:
- "From 30% post-harvest loss to 6%. One system made the difference."
- "Delivery time was 5 days. Now it's 18 hours."
- "Two years ago I couldn't sell 100kg. Last week I sold 2 tonnes."

Best for: LinkedIn, TikTok (transformation content performs well).
Risk: Low — transformations are compelling and verifiable.

**Framework 9: The Social Proof**
Lead with evidence that others are already doing/believing something.

Template: `[Number] [people/companies] already [do thing]. Here's why.`

Psychology: Social proof reduces perceived risk. If many others do it,
the reader assumes it's been validated.

Examples:
- "400+ farms switched to direct-to-consumer this year."
- "Every logistics expert I've talked to says the same thing."
- "3 of the top 5 agritech companies use this approach."

Best for: LinkedIn, Facebook. Trust-building platforms.
Risk: Low — numbers must be accurate. Inflated social proof destroys trust.

**Framework 10: The Challenge**
Dare the reader to test something or reconsider their approach.

Template: `Try this for [timeframe] and tell me it doesn't work.`

Psychology: Challenges activate competitiveness and agency. The reader
imagines taking the challenge before finishing the post.

Examples:
- "Track your post-harvest loss for one week. I bet you'll be shocked."
- "Try selling directly to 5 restaurants this month."
- "Delete your lowest-performing crop and focus on your top 3."

Best for: X, TikTok (interactive platforms). Facebook community engagement.
Risk: Medium — the challenge must be realistic and useful.

**Framework 11: The "What I Learned"**
Signal a hard-won insight from personal experience.

Template: `After [experience/timeframe], here's what I learned about [topic].`

Psychology: Experience-based insights carry authority. The reader trusts
that the writer has earned this knowledge.

Examples:
- "After 14 months building a farm marketplace, here's what nobody told me."
- "I visited 40 farms in 6 months. The pattern was always the same."
- "3 years of delivery data taught me one thing about timing."

Best for: LinkedIn (thought leadership), founder/builder audiences.
Risk: Low — vulnerability and honesty consistently drive engagement.

**Framework 12: The Against the Grain**
Reveal that you deliberately do the opposite of convention — and it works.

Template: `Everyone says [conventional]. I do the opposite. Here's why.`

Psychology: Similar to contrarian but more personal. Curiosity about why
someone would intentionally deviate and succeed.

Examples:
- "Everyone says diversify crops. I planted only one thing this year."
- "Most platforms charge commission. We don't take a single shilling."
- "Industry experts say scale up. We deliberately stayed small."

Best for: Brand differentiation, founder philosophy posts.
Risk: Medium — the unconventional approach must have produced real results.

### Step 3: Draft 3 Hook Variants

For each post, draft at least 3 variants using different frameworks.
For each variant, note:
- Framework used
- Psychological trigger
- Character count
- Whether it fits above the fold on the target platform
- Whether it works in isolation (shareable as a standalone line)

### Step 4: Platform-Specific Adaptation

**LinkedIn above-the-fold:** ~210 characters visible before "...see more."
The hook must fit entirely or create irresistible tension to click.
Effective length: 12-18 words, 80-140 characters.

**X scan zone:** The entire tweet is visible, but users scan the first
60-70 characters in a fast-scrolling timeline. Front-load the punch.
Effective length: 8-14 words, 50-100 characters.

**TikTok caption preview:** ~55-65 characters visible before truncation.
Caption hook should echo the video's spoken opening.
Effective length: 6-10 words, 40-80 characters.

**Facebook mobile fold:** ~125 characters visible before "See more."
Optimize for mobile viewing.
Effective length: 10-16 words, 70-130 characters.

### Step 5: Hook-to-Content Alignment Check

The hook makes a promise. The body must keep it. Run these checks:

| Check | Question |
|-------|----------|
| Promise match | Does the body deliver exactly what the hook promises? |
| Tone match | Is the hook's intensity appropriate for the content depth? |
| Specificity match | If the hook uses a number, does the body substantiate it? |
| Audience match | Would the person drawn in by the hook find the body relevant? |
| CTA match | Does the CTA relate to the hook's theme? |

If any check fails, revise the hook to match the content or revise
the content to match the hook.

### Step 6: Hook Intensity Calibration

Match intensity to content weight:

| Content Weight | Hook Intensity | Example |
|---------------|----------------|---------|
| Major announcement | High | "We just changed how 400 farms sell produce. Forever." |
| Educational post | Medium | "3 logistics mistakes costing small farms thousands." |
| Community discussion | Low-medium | "What's the one thing you wish you knew before your first market?" |
| Product update | Medium | "Route optimization saved our pilot farms 23% on delivery." |
| Personal reflection | Low-medium | "Last month I visited a farm that almost closed." |

Over-hyping a minor update damages credibility.
Under-selling a major announcement wastes the moment.

### Step 7: Validation Gates

Before finalizing, run the hook through these tests:

1. **The Friend Test:** Would you text this to a friend? If it sounds weird
   out of context, it's too abstract.
2. **The Scroll Test:** Imagine seeing this in a feed of 50 posts. Would you stop?
3. **The Promise Test:** Can the body deliver on what this hook implies?
4. **The Cringe Test:** Read it out loud. If you cringe, it's trying too hard.
5. **The Screenshot Test:** If someone screenshots just this line and shares it,
   does it reflect well on your brand?

## 6. Platform-Specific Guidelines

### 6.1 LinkedIn

- First-person insights perform best ("I spent 14 months...")
- Specific numbers in the first line ("73% of small farms...")
- Short opener + blank line for dramatic pause
- Contrarian and "what I learned" frameworks are top performers
- Avoid: "I'm thrilled to announce...", starting with hashtags

### 6.2 X (Twitter)

- Bold, concise — every word must earn its place
- Thread openers must work as standalone tweets
- Hot takes and proof hooks perform best
- Front-load value in first 60 characters
- Avoid: multi-clause sentences, "Thread:" without a hook

### 6.3 TikTok

- Caption hook should echo the video's spoken hook
- Searchable keyword phrases improve discovery
- Curiosity gaps drive watch-through completion
- Short, informal, high-energy language
- Avoid: formal language, long captions, generic phrases

### 6.4 Facebook

- Questions that invite personal stories drive highest engagement
- Community-oriented, warm language
- Story hooks with relatable setups
- Conversational tone (Facebook is personal, not professional)
- Avoid: LinkedIn-style professional hooks, impersonal third-person

## 7. Quality Checklist

- [ ] Hook is within platform character budget
- [ ] Hook works in complete isolation (standalone)
- [ ] Hook uses one of the 12 defined frameworks
- [ ] Psychological trigger is identified
- [ ] Hook intensity matches content weight
- [ ] Hook-to-content alignment passes all 5 checks
- [ ] At least 3 variants drafted using different frameworks
- [ ] Hook fits "above the fold" on target platform
- [ ] Passes Friend, Scroll, Promise, Cringe, and Screenshot tests
- [ ] No corporate jargon or banned openers
- [ ] Does not overpromise relative to body content
- [ ] Tone matches the platform's native voice

## 9. Complete Examples

### Example 1: LinkedIn Post — Farm Marketplace Launch

**Brief:** Announce marketplace to agricultural entrepreneurs.

Draft 1 (Proof):
```
73% of small farms lose money selling through middlemen.
```
56 chars. Strong specificity. Loss aversion trigger. Works standalone. ✓

Draft 2 (Story):
```
Last week, a farmer told me she drives 4 hours to sell produce at a loss.
```
73 chars. Empathy trigger. Scene-setting. Works standalone. ✓

Draft 3 (Contrarian):
```
The problem with farming isn't growing food. It's selling it.
```
62 chars. Reframes narrative. Clean, quotable. ✓

**Selected:** Draft 1 (Proof) primary, Draft 3 (Contrarian) A/B variant.
**Rationale:** Proof hook provides immediate credibility with loss aversion.
Contrarian reframes the problem for audiences who respond to insight over data.

### Example 2: X Thread — Delivery Optimization

**Brief:** Thread about farm delivery logistics improvement.

Draft 1 (Before/After):
```
Our delivery time was 5 days. Now it's 18 hours.
```
49 chars. Dramatic transformation. Perfect for X. ✓

Draft 2 (List Promise):
```
4 logistics mistakes costing farms thousands. Thread:
```
54 chars. Actionable promise. Clear expectation. ✓

Draft 3 (What I Learned):
```
We tracked every delivery for 6 months. Here's what we found:
```
61 chars. Experience authority. Curiosity gap. ✓

**Selected:** Draft 1 (Before/After) primary.
**Rationale:** Highest stopping power due to dramatic numerical contrast.

## 10. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It's Bad | Better Approach |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| "We're excited to announce..." | Zero hook value, instantly skippable | Start with the result or insight |
| Starting with a hashtag | Hashtags aren't hooks, they're metadata | Lead with a human-readable statement |
| Hook needs context to understand | Feed readers have zero context | Hook must work in complete isolation |
| Overpromising in the hook | Body can't deliver, trust destroyed | Calibrate intensity to content depth |
| Same framework every post | Audience fatigue, formulaic feel | Rotate across all 12 frameworks |
| Multi-sentence hooks | One line = one hook; multiple dilutes impact | Compress to a single tight statement |
| Hooks that sound like ads | "Introducing the revolutionary..." = skip | Frame around the reader's problem |
| Burying the hook in paragraph 2 | First drafts often warm up; delete it | Strongest line must be line one |
| Not testing variants | No data on what works for your audience | Draft 3+ variants and compare |
| Clickbait curiosity gaps | "You won't BELIEVE..." with weak payoff | Genuine curiosity with specific details |
| Copying trending templates verbatim | "I quit my job and..." is overdone | Adapt framework structure with original content |

## 11. Advanced Techniques

### The Nested Hook
Use two hook layers: one in text, one in the image or first carousel slide.
Text hook and visual hook complement but don't duplicate each other.

### The Anti-Hook
Radical simplicity in a feed of hyperbolic hooks:
- "Here's what we built. Here's why." (amidst "This changes EVERYTHING")
Works best for established brands with audience trust.

### Emotional Layering
Combine two psychological triggers for stronger hooks:
- Curiosity + loss aversion: "There's a reason your best produce never reaches market."
- Social proof + aspiration: "400 farms figured this out. You can too."
- Specificity + surprise: "We tracked 312 deliveries. The data shocked us."
Stick to two triggers — three creates overload.

### Seasonal Hook Amplifiers
Tie hooks to timely context for 20-40% engagement boost:
- "It's harvest week and most farms still don't know [insight]."
- "2026 taught me one thing about farming: [lesson]."

### The Callback Hook
Reference a previous popular post:
- "Remember when I posted about the 73% middleman loss? 200 replies."
Creates continuity and leverages existing audience investment.

## 13. Notes

- Hooks are written before the body but may be revised after the body is
  complete to ensure alignment. Hook-first ensures maximum impact; flexibility
  to adjust prevents misalignment.
- When a human reviewer rejects a hook, generate 3 new variants using
  different frameworks from the original. Do not reword the same approach.
- Track hook framework performance over time. After 50+ posts, data will
  show which frameworks drive the highest engagement for your audience.
  Feed this back into framework selection.
- For multilingual campaigns, hooks must be written natively in the target
  language, not translated. Hook structures depend on rhetoric and word order.
